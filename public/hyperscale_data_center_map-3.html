Five research dimensions

Growth

28.9% revenue growth — highest on initial shortlist. AI demand for GPU compute and storage is the primary driver, with Goldman Sachs projecting a 165% increase in power demand by 2030.

Hiring demand

Broad but limited permanent count. Construction, electrical, HVAC, grid, cybersecurity, network, and facility operations roles benefit most. Permanent headcount per facility is small.

Data accessibility

Public data exists for energy, emissions, water stress, location, incentives, and labor. Company-level water use, exact workloads, and private energy contracts may be hard to access.

Pain points

Electricity demand, water stress, cooling, grid reliability, land use, noise, diesel backup generators, community opposition, tax incentives, and whether public benefits match public costs.

Opportunity

An AI Data Center Impact Dashboard to help users compare locations, power demand, water risk, cooling approach, incentives, jobs, tax benefit, and community burden. Core question: What does AI infrastructure cost in electricity, water, land, cooling, incentives, and community impact?

Public benefits claimed

  • Large capital investment (billions per campus)
  • Construction employment + local spending
  • Tax base expansion in counties
  • Grid + fiber infrastructure upgrades
  • Technology economy positioning
  • Rural development in low-density areas

Public risks + tradeoffs

  • Power demand strains local utility grids
  • Water stress in drought-prone states
  • Diesel generator emissions + noise
  • Large tax breaks for limited permanent jobs
  • Indirect displacement + land pressure
  • Infrastructure costs shifted to ratepayers

Employment + salary landscape

Hyperscale data centers are capital-heavy, power-heavy, and land-heavy — but not labor-heavy after construction. Permanent staffing is typically modest compared with land, electricity, water, and tax incentives involved.

Construction phase
Hundreds–thousands of temporary jobs: electricians, HVAC, concrete crews, welders, fiber installers, power-line workers, security, project managers.
Operations phase
Smaller permanent headcount — highly automated. DC technicians, critical facilities, network support, security, ops managers, vendor contractors.
Policy tension
Dashboard must separate construction vs. permanent jobs and calculate incentives per permanent job — not just total announced investment.

State incentives + legal landscape

38 states offer dedicated data center tax incentives (NCSL). Key public-finance question: Is the state giving away too much tax revenue for too few permanent jobs?

Community burden + displacement risk

Data centers can create indirect displacement and environmental justice concerns through land pressure, rezoning, utility-rate increases, water competition, noise, diesel emissions, traffic, and public infrastructure costs.

Direct displacement risk

Land acquisition, rezoning, utility easements, transmission corridors, and road or water infrastructure may affect residents and landowners.

Indirect displacement risk

Rising land values, higher utility costs, and industrialization of rural or low-cost areas can pressure lower-income communities.
